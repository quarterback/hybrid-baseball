Task — Talent Distribution Tiers
What & Why
The current player-generation logic produces a too-flat attribute distribution. Every team’s roster looks roughly identical, doubles and triples are sparse, and there are no genuine standout players. Real baseball talent is sharply tiered — a small number of elite players carry outsized impact, and a long tail of replacement-level players keeps the league functional.
Generate players against a defined talent distribution, applied per attribute, with the percentages below. The 20–80 scout scale is the unit; tier boundaries map to scout-scale ranges.
Tier definitions



|Tier           |% of league|Scout scale range|Real-baseball comp          |
|---------------|-----------|-----------------|----------------------------|
|Elite          |2%         |75–80            |MVP / Cy Young candidates   |
|Excellent      |5%         |65–74            |All-Stars                   |
|Very Good      |10%        |60–64            |Above-average regulars      |
|Good           |15%        |55–59            |Solid regulars              |
|Above Average  |18%        |50–54            |League-average starters     |
|Average        |20%        |45–49            |Bench / spot starters       |
|Below Average  |15%        |40–44            |Marginal big leaguers       |
|Replacement    |10%        |30–39            |AAAA / fringe               |
|Sub-Replacement|5%         |20–29            |Wouldn’t be on a real roster|

Total: 100%.
Application
The distribution applies per attribute, not per player. A player can be Elite in Power but Below Average in Speed. Real players are spiky. Generating each attribute independently against this distribution produces realistic player archetypes naturally:
	•	A player who rolls Elite Contact + Below Average Power = Tony Gwynn-type
	•	Elite Power + Below Average Contact = Adam Dunn-type
	•	Elite Speed + Average Contact = Billy Hamilton-type
	•	Elite across the board = once-in-a-generation player (will be vanishingly rare given the multiplication of probabilities)
For a 30-team league with 17-player rosters (510 players), the expected counts per attribute:
	•	~10 Elite
	•	~26 Excellent
	•	~51 Very Good
	•	~77 Good
	•	~92 Above Average
	•	~102 Average
	•	~77 Below Average
	•	~51 Replacement
	•	~26 Sub-Replacement
Pitcher attributes
Same distribution applies to: Stuff, Control, Movement, Stamina.
A pitcher with Elite Stamina is a true workhorse — these are the pitchers whose AOR should average 24–26 across the season. Elite Stuff produces high K rates. The combination of the two = ace.
Hitter attributes
Same distribution applies to: Contact, Power, Eye, Speed, Defense, Arm.
Power is the attribute that drives doubles, triples, and home runs. Right now those are sparse because Power is too uniform. With ~10 Elite Power hitters across the league, you’ll see HR leaders posting 30+ HR seasons and doubles leaders pushing 50+. Speed drives triples specifically — Elite Speed + average Power produces triple specialists.
Implementation
In the player generator (probably o27v2/league.py or similar), replace whatever current attribute generation looks like with:

TALENT_TIERS = [
    (0.02, (75, 80)),   # Elite
    (0.05, (65, 74)),   # Excellent
    (0.10, (60, 64)),   # Very Good
    (0.15, (55, 59)),   # Good
    (0.18, (50, 54)),   # Above Average
    (0.20, (45, 49)),   # Average
    (0.15, (40, 44)),   # Below Average
    (0.10, (30, 39)),   # Replacement
    (0.05, (20, 29)),   # Sub-Replacement
]

def roll_attribute(rng):
    r = rng.random()
    cumulative = 0
    for prob, (lo, hi) in TALENT_TIERS:
        cumulative += prob
        if r < cumulative:
            return rng.randint(lo, hi)
    return rng.randint(20, 29)  # safety


Roll each attribute independently for each player.
Verification
After regenerating the league, the leaderboard should produce:
	•	HR leader posting 30+ HR (top ~5–10 hitters in the 25–35 range)
	•	Doubles leader posting 45+ 2B
	•	Triples leader posting 12+ 3B (still rare but visible)
	•	Top batting average around .380–.420 (Elite Contact)
	•	Top OPS over 1.100
	•	ERA leader well below league average (~6.50–7.50 vs league ~11.50)
	•	K leader posting genuinely high totals
	•	A clear separation between top-of-leaderboard and middle-of-leaderboard
Sanity check: spot-check 10 random players. Some should look genuinely bad (multiple sub-replacement attributes), some should look genuinely great. If every roll-up looks similar, the distribution isn’t being applied correctly.
Out of scope
	•	Position-specific distributions (catchers usually have lower speed in real baseball, etc.) — keep it uniform across positions for v1.
	•	Age curves or attribute drift across seasons — players are static for now.
	•	Generating “famous player” archetypes by name — keep generation procedural.
